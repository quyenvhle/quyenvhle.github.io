Automating Autodesk Reporting Process


During the Summer of 2019, I interned on Autodesk's Global Customer Events team as a Metrics & Reporting Automation Specialist Intern. My goal was to help reduce manual workload of Autodesk University, the company's annual 11,000-attendee conference, through identifying opportunities where our team can use automation to get the right data to the right people.

Problem

People within the event team spend a lot of time on gathering, analyzing, and distributing registration data. On the other hand, people from the product groups and marketers, who want access to this information to better come up with strategies, have to go through back-and-forth communication with the system admin to get what they want. This results in a waste of approximately 60 working hours per month that can be used for something else.

Approach

I tackled this problem with a four-phase process.


Stakeholder Interviews

During the first three weeks of my internship, I spent almost every day interviewing stakeholders. After several conversations with 31 people, I gained insights on how the current process was like, what the stakeholders' goals were, what pain points these stakeholders were facing and was able to identify the project scope. I also analyzed how different data sources were connected together and was able to produce an integrated process flow and a database architecture diagram.

Solution Evaluation

After gathering and reviewing requirements, I conducted brainstorming sessions and came up with many potential solutions. With a few criteria, including accessibility and feasibility, I filtered these options down to an ultimate one: using a Power BI dashboard.

Build, Break, Build Again

Now that a solution was found, the next question was: "How do we automate?". To enable automation, I wrote Python scripts that grabbed data out of APIs, modeled the heavily-nested data to be production-ready, and loaded it into Power BI for visualization. The scripts are scheduled to run on a daily basis so the dashboard always stayed up-to-date.



User Testing & Enhancement

I tested the dashboard with my direct team as well as presented it to senior leadership. After some final touches, the dashboard was officially launched during the first week of September and received positive feedback from people within the company.


Impact

The dashboard significantly improves efficiency by:
• Reducing manual works by 60 hours: eliminate the hours spent on report generation, back-and-forth communication, and error correction.
• Increasing data visibility to 200+ stakeholders (as of September 2019): ensure important data is accessible when needed.
• Cutting down process time to 15 minutes average: have the most relevant metrics ready.

What's Next

Beyond the dashboard, I also took the time to perform some exploratory analysis to better understand attendee engagement during the conference. This allowed me to come up with six uncovered metrics and pitched these to a team in charged of building an internal marketing intelligence tool. Although the team loved the metrics, they weren't able comprehensively paint the big picture of how Autodesk customers and business partners interact with different marketing and sales channels since they only reflect what's coming from the Events side.

As I will come back as a Data Analytics Intern for the fall semester, I hope to further look at engagement data pre- and post-conference, as well as additional data from Sales and Marketing, to refine these metrics and potentially implement a machine learning model to measure engagement.

Reflection

Overall, the internship was a spectacular experience for me. I challenged myself with learning Python on the spot, volunteered with Girls Who Code, engaged in talks with product managers and data scientists around the Bay, tried laser cutting for the first time, and of course, slurped copious amount of Philz coffee which was the-best-thing-ever.

Two critical lessons I learned:
• Work independently but collaboratively: With full ownership of this project, I managed my own timeline and deliverables, but at the same time, needed to stay communicative with stakeholders to ensure they know about the dashboard and its impact.
• Stay proactive: As the only technical person within my team, there were times that I got stuck with a piece of code or didn't know if my analysis was right. Thus, I had to actively meet with data analysts and engineers from other departments and asked them questions to help me move forward.
