Enhancing The MailChimp Experience


This was an exercise I did last winter when I applied to the Product Management Internship at MailChimp, a marketing automation platform and email services. The application did not require one; however, if I was serious about becoming a Product Manager, I must first know about the ins and outs of the product, its competitors, and opportunities for improvement.

Industry Analysis

Since I was not familiar with marketing automation, I started off by conducting an industry analysis. It seems that the industry is a promising one, as email becomes the most important means of communication these days. It is also a competitive one, with many small and midsize players with minimal product differentiation. Thus, companies in this industry heavily rely on marketing to get customers.

Competitive Analysis

Although MailChimp is the top player with 62.12% market share, there is a threat of takeover as competitors are acquiring each other to diversify their product offerings and at the same time, increase marketing to the public

Recommendations

As marketing can longer be something MailChimp should rely on, I believe the company should ensure that it matches industry standard by finding out what important features others have that it doesn't, and at the same time, focus on differentiating its product with additional features.

Visual Workflow

Email marketers love leads, especially those they spend adequate amount of resources to capture and retain. In fact, nurtured leads perform 47% larger purchases than first-timers. Most email marketers rely on automation for leads nurturing, as automated emails receive 152% higher open rates than non-automated ones.

To help marketers better manage their automated campaigns, companies within the industry offer visual workflow builder. This is a graphical user interface with which marketers can create workflows by dragging and dropping processes and rules. However, compared to its competitors' offerings, MailChimp's workflow builder receives much lower score.

Why does MailChimp's score fall on the lower end? As I reviewed different workflow builders, I noticed that MailChimp's embraces a list-based layout, not a visual one. Therefore, marketers have to scroll down if they want to view every node of their workflows, which may be discouraging. Additionally, the list-based workflow is not very user-friendly in "if-else" scenarios.

The fact that visuals play a more effective role than text in the decision making process of humans also suggests that visual workflow is something MailChimp need to improve upon. By providing marketers with this feature, MailChimp can help them better monitor automated campaigns and gain visibility in key nodes with high impact (as well as problematic ones). This will result in MailChimp not falling short behind its competitors anymore.

Propensity Modelling

Another area I think MailChimp should consider is to incorporate propensity modelling into its analytics capabilities. Propensity modeling is one of the most important predictive analytics technique, along with cluster modeling (segmentation) and collaborative filtering (recommendation). It is used to anticipate the future behaviors of customers based on past behaviors. There are four main types of propensity modeling: propensity to engage, propensity to churn, propensity to purchase, and propensity to unsubscribe.

Based on research, 23% of marketers said that they employ some forms of predictive analysis in their marketing strategy, while 32% wish to implement within the next five years. That being said, marketers care about what actions their customers would take in the future. The rise of big data has create numerous opportunities to understand customers, but at the same time security regulations arise, making future data less accessible. Therefore, marketers must learn to leverage past and current data to craft successful strategy.

Up until now, MailChimp has only tackled propensity to engage with the Send Time Optimization feature which tells marketer the best time to send out emails, and propensity to purchase through Canopy Labs integration, which calculates customer lifetime value and the probabilities of repurchase.

Thus, there exist opportunities for MailChimp to take advantage of propensity to churn and propensity to unsubscribe, providing marketers with a more comprehensive picture of customers. This can be done by building an in-house capability to predict churn/unsubscription, or allowing more integration options with churn analytics platforms such as Dataiku. As none of other industry players have a similar capability, adopting these models will help MailChimp differentiates and increases its competitive advantage.

Next Steps

Due to time constraint, I ended up not being able to prototype these two recommendations, but I plan to continue iterating. I did send the inital powerpoint to some product managers of MailChimp and truly hope that they will take my suggestions into consideration when enhancing and optimizing the platform.

Reflection

Through the exercise, I realized that usage data is extremely important in building products that can address the desired needs of users. My recommendations are merely based on the data I gathered from the Internet and my assumptions, but what if real usage data shows the other way? Therefore, a great product manager must learn to not take data for granted. I would like to wrap up with a quote from Tomas Chamorro-Premuzic:
“Purely intuitive managers may face extinction only if they ignore the valuable information provided by data. At the same time, managers who are capable of data-driven intuition will remain in demand, and increasingly so.”
