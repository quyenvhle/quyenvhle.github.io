Expediting Website Creation For Vitris


With all the "Where did you intern at?" questions going on at the beginning of junior year, I could not help but felt stressful. I decided that I must made up for the summer by having an internship during the fall, so I ended up shooting my cover letter and portfolio to Jack Perrotta, founder of Vitris. The journey turned out to be more than what I expected: although filled with ups and downs, startup life definitely shaped me as a person today.

Background

Situated at MakeOffices co-working space in the heart of Philadelphia, Vitris is a digital marketing agency specialized in websites, online paid advertising and content creation. Started as a phone repair entrepreneurship venture from Temple University, Vitris has grown into a company which utilized artificial intelligence to help small business owners succeed in the online world.

One of the factor that helps Vitris differentiate itself is its in-house content management system, called the Vitris platform. Like Wordpress and Wix, this system allows Vitris to build customized websites for customers, but at the same time, integrate appointment booking, online payments, analytics reporting and local search.

My Role

If I could use one line to describe what I did, I would say that "I was always on the quest for the best ideas that help optimize the Vitris platform". Even though that my title was Product Intern, I also got to be in the shoes of developers and designers to look at those ideas from different perspectives.

Understanding The Back End

Two hours into my first day, I had my first project: validating the use of code variables across the platform. At that time, the team has no standard way to document the codebase, which resulted in variables misused and bugs unable to trace. Jack thought that giving me this task will help me onboard more easily.

Indeed, over the next days, my Chrome tabs blew up. I spent hours familiarizing myself with TypeScript and Angular.js. I also printed out over 100 pages of code and used my highlighter to map variables and figured out how they were used. I would not say that I liked this project; however, it definitely allowed me to know the ins and outs of the platform.

My deliverable, a source-code documentation, ended up helping the team reduce duplication, errors and redundancy. It was a good start. I was ready for the next steps.

Front End Here We Go

The following week, I rotated to the Design team and undertook three web design projects, for real customers. I shadowed the Lead Designer, hopped on phone calls with customers to understand the brand, researched the best practices in color mixing, used Adobe Photoshop/Illustrator to create customized components, and most importantly, learned how the web renderer portion of Vitris platform works.

Being in the shoes of designers who used the platform daily, I realized that the whole process of creating a website was super lengthy. It was due to the fact that the platform prompts designers to reupload every time they want to reuse an image. Curious, I observed other designers in the company and noticed that this is a common struggle.

I wondered if there was a better way to manage media files so that the whole process could be expedited?

Getting My Hands Dirty With Product Management

I conducted a competitive analysis between Vitris and other successful CMSs, namely WordPress, Wix and Weebly.

In addition to the analysis, I compiled a pitch deck detailing the needs for and the impact of having a centralized media library and presented to the team. There were questions about whether or not this was an immediate need. However, I argued that not only the feature would help designers save time for other things, but also benefit customers since Vitris wanted to hand over the web design process to them in the long-run. Higher customer satisfaction and increased competitive advantage.

My proposal got approved. A project to build the feature was created, and I went on to the prototyping phase.

Prototyping

I created some low-fidelity prototype for:
• The media library standing alone.
• The media library popping up when users want to import an image into their websites.

Implementation & Results

After one month, we finished implementing the Media Library. The additional feature helped simplify the process of uploading and using image files and accelerated the web design process from an average of 9 days to 7 days.



Other Projects

Apart from the media library, I also contributed to the following projects as a product manager:
• Developed product roadmaps through analyzing market trends, competitive landscape and user data.
• Performed comperative analysis and created a proof-of-concept for the integration of an inline rich text editor.
• Prototyped the blog feature for the Vitris platform.
• Standardized UI components (buttons, cards, accordion).

Reflection

My five months with Vitris was valuable. As I've always been a person that prefers structure and clear layers, I learned to twerk the old working style to embrace the uncertainties. In a startup, people do things outside of their realms: a designer can code and a developer must learn to design; thus, this experience has helped me become more versatile and developed a growth mindset of always keep exploring.

Startup life is also not very easy. There's always a battle between releasing products to get customers as early as possible (immediate success), and taking longer time to ensure that the right products are out there (sustainable success). There's pressure to break new ground, but there's also pressure to not discourage employees. Either way, it's necessary to self-cultivate an energy that keeps you rolling up your sleeves and getting your hands dirty. In the end, it will pay off eventually.
